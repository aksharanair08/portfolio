INTERFACE DESIGN FOR AV'S

Trust in AVs - Cultivating Trust Between Young Drivers and Autonomous Vehicles Through User-Centered Design

About the Project

To foster trust, it's important to consider insights from research papers. One of the research papers highlights the significance of addressing human-related factors, such as driver fatigue and poor health, which contribute to 75% of traffic accidents. To mitigate these risks, an innovative approach is recommended, which includes features such as automatic vehicle speed control through an autonomous braking system, sleepiness detection, and driver health monitoring. This can enhance safety and prevent errors.

Another research paper, which used traffic microsimulation and the surrogate safety assessment model, revealed that AVs can result in a substantial 12% reduction in the annual crash rate. This highlights the potential for AVs to significantly improve overall road safety. Additionally, a study emphasized on the importance of user-centric design elements, including aesthetics and usability, in shaping trust and distrust in AV systems.

Positive user experiences, driven by pragmatic quality, need fulfillment, and aesthetic appeal, play a crucial role in fostering trust. In summary, a holistic design approach that prioritizes safety features, user-friendly interfaces, and aesthetic appeal is necessary to build trust among young drivers in embracing autonomous technology.

Design Challenge

How might we cultivate trust between Young Drivers and Autonomous Vehicles?

Recap

Define

During the data analysis phase, we used Miro to visually organize codes into color-coded categories that emerged as key study themes.

"Factors Influencing Driving Ability" included distractions, multiple passengers, sleep patterns, eyesight issues, stress-related crashes, and external conditions affecting performance. "Safety Concerns with AVs" highlighted mistrust in braking, skepticism about auto-detection, and doubts about alerts, especially in low visibility. "Driving Behavior" revealed insights into speed control, emotion regulation, and risky tendencies. "New Safety Features" captured preferences for communication tools, voice alerts, warnings, and SOS options in autonomous vehicles. Finally, "Driving Experience" addressed concerns about adverse conditions, night driving, and overall driving encounters.

This structured approach provided nuanced insights to inform further research and design decisions.

Personas

Ideation – Crazy 8s

With this scenario in mind, we identified common concerns among users, we crafted insight statements, and formulated "How might we" statements to distill these concerns into concrete problem statements. For each problem statement, we brainstormed design solutions using the Crazy 8s method. For a total of eight minutes, each team member generated eight designs averaging about a minute per design. Encouraging each team member to rapidly sketch various ideas as potential solutions to these problem statements.

Next, each team member chose several favorites to present to the group as possible solutions to our design challenge. Following the sketching phase, a comprehensive discussion ensued, during which we evaluated and ranked the ideas through a voting process. This collaborative approach allowed us to pinpoint and select the most promising solutions for addressing our defined problem statements. The final refined ideas for addressing user concerns about Autonomous Vehicles (AVs) center around communication, reliability, and assistance, which can be further categorized into Infotainment and Mobile Application features.

Our project aims to build trust in young drivers using this two-fold approach. Our proposed design solutions are:

Delayed Transition and Clear Communication

Recognizing the challenges posed by heavy snowfall, the infotainment system communicates with the driver and our system proactively alerts the driver to assume manual control. A prominent visual alert, accompanied by an audio cue, signals the imminent need for intervention. To emphasize urgency, a countdown timer is displayed, highlighting the remaining time before manual control becomes necessary. In the event the driver fails to take charge, the AI co-pilot intervenes, guiding the vehicle to a secure location and prioritizing safety.

Optional HUD and Hands-Free Commands

Implementing an optional Heads-Up Display (HUD) screen that projects essential information directly into the driver's field of vision, eliminating the need to divert attention. The optional nature of this feature gives users greater control, allowing them to choose whether or not to use it based on personal preferences. Our design further integrates hands-free commands, granting users the flexibility to enable the Heads-Up Display (HUD) effortlessly at any moment. This intuitive approach empowers users to choose the specific information they wish to view, enabling navigation directions or HUD with a simple voice command.

Optimized Assistive Features

Leveraging an AI co-pilot to analyze the driver's travel history and saved data, offering personalized route recommendations and suggestions for a more tailored and efficient driving experience. This approach aims to enhance the overall assistive capabilities of the AV system. In addition, the infotainment system offers ‘Custom modes’ where users can easily store and apply preferred settings for various aspects such as seat position, climate control, music, lighting, apps, and volume. This can be accomplished through a single voice command, providing users with a seamless and personalized driving experience.

Build Network

Developing a user-friendly mobile app driven by community interaction and feedback. This app allows users to access and share vehicle efficiency and statistical data, including trip history and essential statistics. By establishing a network that encourages collaboration and data sharing, the goal is to build trust among young drivers using automated vehicles.

Prototyping Iteration – Low fidelity

At the end of the ideation and sketching phase, our key features had been identified and the next phase was to build out the prototypes and test our ideas. Our approach was contingent on the fact that we would be testing these features outside of an actual AV. It was imperative to simulate the feeling of being in an AV, performing driving and non-driving tasks in a level 3 AV particularly while using the infotainment functions. Our prototype iterations can be divided into three phases: Low-fidelity, high-fidelity, and iterated high-fidelity prototypes.

To begin with, we first developed low-fidelity prototypes that employed the use of sketches to convey the layout and features in both the infotainment screen and the mobile application. This step helped us decide which layouts and features work best in the proposed solutions. We tested multiple layouts and conducted within-team discussions to come to a consensus before moving to high-fidelity prototypes.

Next, we developed high-fidelity prototypes, our high-fidelity screens were built with the Figma platform.

Results

What improvements or additional features would you suggest for both the infotainment interface and the mobile application?

  • Gesture Control
  • Mixed Reality Navigation
  • An inside view into the driving software decision-making process
  • Emergency and SOS Phone Numbers
  • Road Assistance anywhere in the country, and multiple device car tracking
  • Remote Control Features
  • Personalized Notifications and Alerts
  • Information on the availability and locations of nearby EV charging points, at a lower cost on the app

Summary

The survey shows a strong preference for customizable modes and robust audio-visual alerts, highlighting the value users place on personalization and safety. The vehicle statistics feature in the mobile app was seen as particularly useful, reflecting interest in real-time vehicle data. 70% of users believe combining the infotainment system and mobile app is most effective, suggesting a synergistic approach builds trust in autonomous vehicle technology. The overall UI design of infotainment systems received high satisfaction (5/5), indicating positive reception of current designs.
